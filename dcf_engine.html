(1)
Intrinsic Value — DCF Sum
V= n∑t = 1 FCFt(1 + r)t
Sum of each period's free cash flow discounted to the present at rate r (WACC), over an explicit forecast horizon of n years.
(2)
Terminal Value — Gordon Growth Model
TV= FCFn · (1 + g)r − g
Capitalises all cash flows beyond year n as a growing perpetuity at long-run rate g. Requires g < r.
(3)
Present Value of Terminal Value
PV(TV)= TV(1 + r)n
Discounts the terminal value back from year n to today.
(4)
Enterprise Value
EV= n∑t = 1 FCFt(1 + r)t + TV(1 + r)n
Total firm value to all capital providers — the sum of equations (1) and (3).
(5)
Intrinsic Price per Share
P= EV − D + CS
Equity value per share, where D = total debt, C = cash & equivalents, and S = shares outstanding.
(6)
Free Cash Flow
FCF= Operating Cash Flow−Capital Expenditures
Cash generated by the business after funding reinvestment — the truest measure of a firm's economic earnings.

Input Terms — What You're Setting
gRevenue Growth Rate
The annual rate at which you expect the company's free cash flow to grow over the next 5 years. It is the single most impactful assumption in the model — a higher growth rate produces a dramatically higher valuation. Be conservative. Most mature companies grow FCF at 5–10% annually. High-growth companies might justify 15–25%, but the higher you go, the more you need to justify it with actual evidence.
Typical ranges: Mature/stable 3–8% · Growth company 10–20% · Hypergrowth 20–30%
rWACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital
WACC is the blended required rate of return across all of the company's capital — both equity (shareholders) and debt (lenders). It acts as the discount rate — it answers "what return would I need to earn elsewhere to justify this investment?" A higher WACC shrinks the valuation; a lower WACC inflates it.
Typical ranges: Low-risk blue chip 7–9% · Mid-cap 9–12% · Small/risky company 12–16%
g∞Terminal Growth Rate
After the 5-year explicit forecast, the model assumes the business grows at a perpetual rate forever. This feeds into the Gordon Growth Model to produce the terminal value. It must always be below WACC, and should never exceed long-run nominal GDP growth (~2–3%), since no company can outgrow the entire economy indefinitely.
Typical ranges: 1.5–2.5% for most companies · Never above 3.5% · Must be < WACC
Output Terms — What the Model Returns
FCFFree Cash Flow
Free cash flow is the cash a business generates after paying all its operating costs and reinvesting in itself. Unlike accounting profit, FCF is very hard to manipulate. It represents the true economic output of the business — not what accountants say it earned, but what it actually produced in cash.
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
TVTerminal Value
Terminal value captures all remaining value beyond the forecast window in a single number, by assuming the final year's FCF grows at the terminal growth rate forever. It typically accounts for 60–80% of total enterprise value, which is why the terminal growth rate assumption is so consequential.
⚠ TV usually dominates the valuation — treat it with extra skepticism
MOSMargin of Safety
Coined by Benjamin Graham, the margin of safety is the gap between intrinsic value and market price. It acts as a buffer against errors in your assumptions. Value investors typically require at least 20–30% margin of safety before buying. A negative MOS means the market is already pricing in more optimism than your model justifies.
MOS = (Intrinsic Value − Market Price) / Intrinsic Value
